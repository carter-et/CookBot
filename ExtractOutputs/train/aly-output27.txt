But I have to tell you Pon de Ring is <span style="text-decoration:underline;">NOT</span> Mochi Donut and here’s why.</p> <p><span style="text-decoration:underline;"><strong>Pon de Ring</strong></span></p> <ul> <li>Made of <strong>tapioca</strong> flour and <strong>wheat flour</strong> (officially mentioned by Mister Donut)</li> <li><strong>Soft</strong>, <strong>airy</strong>, <strong>bouncy</strong>, and <strong>chewy</strong></li> </ul> <p><span style="text-decoration:underline;"><strong>Mochi Donut</strong></span></p> <ul> <li>Made of <strong>glutinous rice flour</strong></li> <li><strong>Dense</strong> and <strong>chewy</strong></li> <li>Not sold in Japan, but pretty popular in the US</li> </ul> <p>As you can see, the ingredients and texture of these two donuts are very different. If you have tried Pon de Ring in Japan, you would be able to tell them apart. Even though some of the mochi donuts might look like a copycat of Pon de Ring.</p> <p>In Japanese, we describe Pon de Ring’s mochi-like texture as <em>mochi-mochi</em> (モチモチしてる) or <em>mocchiri</em> (モッチリしている) texture, but it does not always mean that the food being described is made of mochi. For example, bagels with a chewy texture can be described as it has a mochi-like texture.</p> <p>If you have tried and loved the texture of mochi donuts, check out Mandy’s website, <a href="https://ladyandpups.com/2014/06/13/mochi-donuts/">Lady and Pups</a> for the recipe. Our focus from the beginning was to achieve the right airy but mochi-like texture of Pon de Ring. If we can’t make it the same due to lack of machinery or unnatural ingredients, we wanted to at least achieve the best possible quality.</p> <h4>Challenge #1: Choosing the right flour(s)</h4> <p>We started by working out the main ingredients – specifically flours. We tried the following flours in chronological order:</p> <ol> <li><strong>glutinous rice flour only</strong> – It tasted like Mochi Donut, but not the same as Pon de Ring.</li> <li><strong>glutinous rice flour + tofu (with different ratios)</strong> – You’ll find tons of Japanese “Pon de Ring-like (ポンデリング風)” recipes online using the glutinous rice flour and tofu combination, so I had to try at least once. Just like everyone claimed, this is almost <span style="text-decoration:underline;">Pon de Ring-like</span> donuts but not quite right at all. Why tofu, you may ask? Any food made of rice (in this case, glutinous rice flour) becomes hard naturally when it’s cool. Adding tofu (and decreasing rice component in the mixture) helps the texture to stay a bit softer.</li> <li><strong>tapioca flour only</strong> – After further research, I learned that Mister Donut officially said Pon de Ring contains tapioca flour. So I tried making the donut with 100% tapioca flour but couldn’t achieve the right texture.</li> <li><strong>glutinous rice flour + tapioca flour (with 3 different ratios)</strong> – I thought I should combine glutinous rice flour and tapioca flour and see what happens. The glutinous rice made the donuts denser as expected from our trial #1 and #2.</li> <li><strong>all-purpose flour + tapioca flour (with different ratios)</strong> – We researched more. This time we found <a href="https://youtu.be/8VKvA0PwUBw" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">this YouTube video</a> that allowed a camera to go into the Mister Donut’s kitchen. Mr. JOC saw a <span style="text-decoration:underline;">thermometer</span> in the donut dough (see below) and he also remembered seeing a thermometer in the video clip that was looping inside Mister Donut in Japan. <em><strong>This was our turning point!</strong></em> We realized that they are a donut shop after all and also use flour and yeast for Pon de Ring, just like making other donuts! So we tested different ratios of flour and tapioca flour to find out the right texture.</li> <li><strong>bread flour + tapioca flour</strong> – We also tried using bread flour (more gluten) to increase the bouncy texture. The donuts came out with a good mochi-mochi texture but they weren’t airy and light.</li> </ol> <figure aria-describedby="caption-attachment-87409" class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_87409" Instant Yeast</h3> <p><img alt="Instant Dry Yeast" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-87431 nitro-lazy nitro-lazy-empty" data-pin-description="Instant Dry Yeast" data-pin-title="Pon de Ring Donut Recipe ポンデリング" height="533" id="MTU4MjM5MDY2Ni40MTE1" nitro-lazy-src="https://d3eh3svpl1busq.cloudfront.net/KQYMGOLIdXGmoAcyJsPOrQDKktgCbwtG/assets/static/optimized/rev-c031098/wp-content/uploads/2019/09/Instant-Dry-Yeast.jpg" nitro-lazy-srcset="https://d3eh3svpl1busq.cloudfront.net/KQYMGOLIdXGmoAcyJsPOrQDKktgCbwtG/assets/static/optimized/rev-c031098/wp-content/uploads/2019/09/Instant-Dry-Yeast.jpg 800w, https://d3eh3svpl1busq.cloudfront.net/KQYMGOLIdXGmoAcyJsPOrQDKktgCbwtG/assets/static/optimized/rev-c031098/wp-content/uploads/2019/09/Instant-Dry-Yeast-300x200.jpg 300w, https://d3eh3svpl1busq.cloudfront.net/KQYMGOLIdXGmoAcyJsPOrQDKktgCbwtG/assets/static/optimized/rev-c031098/wp-content/uploads/2019/09/Instant-Dry-Yeast-768x512.jpg 768w, https://d3eh3svpl1busq.cloudfront.net/KQYMGOLIdXGmoAcyJsPOrQDKktgCbwtG/assets/static/optimized/rev-c031098/wp-content/uploads/2019/09/Instant-Dry-Yeast-600x400.jpg 600w" nitro-og-height="533" nitro-og-width="800" sizes="(max-width: 800px) 100vw, 800px" width="800"/></p> <p>The two most common dry yeasts are <strong>Instant (Quick/Rapid-Rise) Yeast</strong> and <strong>Active Dry Yeast</strong>. You can read <a href="https://www.seriouseats.com/2018/03/all-about-dry-yeast-instant-active-dry-fast-acting-and-more.html" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">this detailed information</a> about different types of yeasts.</p> <p>The major difference is that active dry yeast always needs to be dissolved in warm liquids (water or milk) before using while instant yeast can be mixed right into the dough. For convenience, I like using Instant Yeast.</p> <p>The two popular instant yeast brands are Red Star and Fleischmann’s and you can find them at grocery stores. I have tried both brands before, and for this recipe, I used Red Star Quick-Rise instant yeast to make my donuts.</p> <p>After reading <a href="https://redstaryeast.com/yeast-baking-lessons/yeast-types-usage/instant-or-fast-rising-yeast/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">this page on instant yeast</a>, I’ve decided to hydrate/dissolve my instant yeast (even though it’s not active dry yeast) in warm milk (110 ºF or 43 ºC – <span style="text-decoration:underline;">slightly</span> warmer than body temperature) to give it a good start. To achieve mochi-mochi texture, the strong gluten needs to be formed. We did try kneading without a stand mixer but the final texture of the donut was not optimal.</li> <li><strong>Try not to add too much flour</strong> – With tapioca flour being the majority of the ingredients, the dough should be soft. To get the ultimate texture for Pon de Ring, try not to add additional flours. I prepared 2 tablespoons of additional all-purpose flour to dust my work surface and my hands. However, I didn’t finish using all the extra flour.</li> <li><strong>Don’t deep fry at high temperature</strong> – The optimal deep-frying temperature for Pon de Ring donuts is between 325 ºF (168 ºC) and  350 ºF (177 ºC). When the oil is too hot, the donuts will turn brown too soon and the texture of the donut skin gets too crispy. When the oil temperature is too low, the donuts don’t look appetizing and absorbs too much oil.</li> <li><strong>Use a paper towel to drain oil</strong> – From my countless recipe testings, I learned that draining oil on sheets of paper towel works better than on a wire rack. After draining, transfer the donuts to a wire rack to cool and glaze.</li> <li><strong>Put glaze when donuts are still hot</strong> – You MUST glaze your Pon de Ring donuts while they are hot! The remaining heat from the donut spreads the glaze beautifully when you flip over. Don’t wait until you finish deep frying all the donuts. Multitask deep frying and glazing at the same time could be hectic so find a partner for this project.</li> </ol> <p>For the donut fans and baking aficionados out there, I hope you would try your hand at Pon de Ring. The donut making was no doubt an endeavor, but we were glad that we perfected the recipe and the reward paid off. Otherwise, add the Pon de Ring donuts to your list of must-eats when you visit Japan.</p> <p><img alt="Homemade glazed and matcha glazed pon de ring donuts on a wire rack." class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-87378 nitro-lazy nitro-lazy-empty" data-pin-description="Here's the copycat recipe for the popular PON DE RING DONUT from Mr. Donut in Japan!
