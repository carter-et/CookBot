Tonjiru as it is more flavorful. The next choice is pork loin slices. If you cannot get ready sliced pork belly or pork loin at local Japanese/Asian grocery stores, you can cut the meat into thin slices or strips yourself (See <a href="https://www.justonecookbook.com/how-to-slice-meat/">the tutorial here</a>).</p> <h3>2. Vegetables</h3> <p>The flavor essence of the soup comes from pork, but it actually features more vegetables. Common vegetables used in Tonjiru are root vegetables such as:</p> <ul> <li>carrots</li> <li>potatoes or satoimo (Japanese taro)</li> <li>daikon (Japanese radish)</li> <li>gobo (burdock root)</li> </ul> <p>Besides the root vegetables, there are other ingredients you can add.</p> <ul> <li>konnyaku (kanjac)</li> <li>tofu or aburaage (deep-fried tofu pouch)</li> <li>shiitake mushrooms or shimeji</li> <li>bean sprouts (my mom always includes them)</li> <li>green onions/spring onions</li> </ul> <p>We do not put leafy vegetables, but if you like to add, say spinach, I recommend blanching them separately and add directly to the soup bowl. This way, you can retain the bright green color and won’t overcook the leafy veggies.</p> <h3>3. Dashi (Japanese soup stock)</h3> <p>Because Tonjiru has a lot of ingredients that contribute to a decent tasting broth, you can get away by using water instead of dashi. However, my choice is always dashi because it gives more depth and umami that pork and vegetables don’t have. You can taste the difference right away.</p> <p>I use the combination of kombu and katsuobushi for dashi (we call <a href="https://www.justonecookbook.com/how-to-make-dashi/">Awase Dashi</a>) in the majority of my cooking, and this recipe is no exception. I highly recommend making homemade dashi, but if you’re in hurry, a <a href="https://www.justonecookbook.com/dashi-packet/">dashi packet</a> from Kayanoya makes a nice quick dashi in no time.</p> <h3>4. Miso (Japanese fermented soybean paste)</h3> <p>There is no specific type of miso you <em>have to</em> use for Tonjiru. If you like one type of miso (such as white miso, awase miso, or red miso), you can use it. If you are new to miso, <a href="https://www.justonecookbook.com/miso/">read this post</a> to get familiar with it.</p> <p>For this Tonjiru, I used <a href="https://hikarimiso.com/product/miso/enjuku-koji-miso/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Enjuku Koji Miso</a> from Hikari Miso®. I love the hint of sweetness that Koji Miso gives.
