data-insertion="after" data-insertion-no-dbg="" data-selector=".ai-insert-2-14311914"></div> <h3><strong>Here’s the Catch and What You Can Do </strong></h3> <p class="p1">Everyone loves tiramisu at the restaurant but I always wondered why I rarely see the homemade versions at potlucks and dinner parties.  Now I know why.</p> <p class="p1">The classic Italian Tiramisu includes raw eggs (both egg yolks and whites).  If I was in Japan, I won’t be too concerned about consuming raw eggs; however, here in the U.S., they are not safe to consume due to the possible risk of salmonella.</p> <p>Now in order to avoid the possible risk, you have a few options.</p> <ul> <li><strong>Option 1:</strong> Use pasteurized eggs (which could be hard to find at your local grocery stores)</li> <li><strong>Option 2:</strong> Pasteurize your own eggs (you can follow <a href="https://www.justonecookbook.com/pasteurize-eggs/">my recipe</a>, but you’ll need an immersion circulator)</li> <li><strong>Option 3:</strong> Swap egg whites with heavy cream (commonly practiced) and <a href="http://www.incredibleegg.org/eggcyclopedia/d/doneness-guidelines/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">cook egg yolks till <strong>160 ­ºF (71 ºC)</strong></a> (not so commonly practiced).</li> </ul> <p class="p1">I thought I’d go with Option 3 as it sounds easier. Next, I’ll talk about what I learned from my experience.</p> <p><img alt="matcha tiramisu cake made with soaked ladyfingers and mascarpone custard filling." class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-89538 simple as I expected, and here’s why.</p> <p>Tiramisu recipe requires a lot of whisking/beating.  You have to beat the egg whites (or heavy cream) and you have to whisk the egg yolk mixture really fast while heating so it won’t become scrambled eggs.  There is no rest in between once you start.</p> <p>At first, I thought my hand whisking would be good enough.  Oh my gosh, after whisking vigorously for close to 10 minutes, I thought my arms were going to fall off.  I had to switch over to my $32 <a href="https://amzn.to/2iM1p0x" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">hand mixer</a> (on sale, regular price $60) which saved my life, seriously.</p> <p>You will also need an <a href="https://www.thermoworks.com/Thermapen-Mk4?tw=j1cb" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">instant-read thermometer</a>. Why such a fancy thermometer?</p> <p>It really comes down to <strong>prompt</strong><b> temperature control </b>when you are mixing the egg mixture. Once the temperature of the egg yolks reaches <strong>160 ­ºF (71 ºC)</strong>, you want to immediately remove from heat and stop it from further cooking. Otherwise, you will end up with scrambled eggs. I appreciate my thermometer even more after making Tiramisu.</p> <p>If you’re a big fan of tiramisu and want to make this recipe frequently, I highly recommend investing in these two kitchen tools.</p> <p><img alt="matcha tiramisu cake made with soaked ladyfingers and mascarpone custard filling." class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-89535 nitro-lazy nitro-lazy-empty" data-pin-description="Matcha Tiramisu - Creamy, rich, and bursting with bold matcha flavors, this irresistible Matcha Tiramisu consists of matcha soaked ladyfingers layered with a light and airy mascarpone custard filling. It’s a classic tiramisu dessert with a Japanese twist!
