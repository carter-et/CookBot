<p>As a child, the anticipation of the holidays meant one thing: My Aunt Patti’s famous sugar cookies. Fluffy, sweet, frosted, perfection.</p>
<p>We would often bake and decorate them together, having plenty of samples along the way. And over the course of the holiday weekend, I would tuck away what felt like dozens of cookies.</p>
<p>I know; not great. But since then, my appetite has matured. I can actually feel a sugar rush coming on and recognize it’s unpleasant, which allows me to enjoy desserts in moderation. A vast improvement for this baker.</p>
<p>Even now as an adult, it doesn’t quite feel like the holidays have arrived unless I bite into a perfectly frosted, fluffy sugar cookie.</p>
<p>Seeing as I’ve already perfected the <a aria-label="vegan, gluten-free sugar cookie (opens in a new tab)" href="https://minimalistbaker.com/fluffy-1-bowl-sugar-cookies-vegan-gf/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">vegan, gluten-free sugar cookie</a>, I wanted to experiment with a grain-free (gluten-free) version to see if it was possible! Spoiler alert: <em>It totally worked</em>.</p>
<p>Let us show you how easy it is to make these<strong> fluffy, gluten- and grain-free 1-bowl</strong> <strong>sugar cookies</strong>!</p>

<h3>How to Make Sugar Cookies</h3>
<p>Traditional sugar cookies are typically made by combining butter, sugar, eggs, and flour into a moldable dough, then baking.</p>
<p>Our version uses a similar method but with slightly different ingredients. We combine dairy-free butter with organic cane sugar, vanilla, baking powder, and one room-temperature egg. </p>
<p>For the flour, we went with: </p>
<ul><li>almond flour for a crumbly, light texture</li><li>arrowroot starch for a bit of structure</li><li>potato starch for light, pillowy fluffiness</li></ul>

<p>This dough does best chilled. Once properly chilled, it can either be scooped out, rolled into balls, and pressed into discs, or rolled out to 1/4-inch thickness and cut out into shapes of choice.</p>
<p>Either way you go, these cookies are delicious. But we happen to prefer the roll-and-cut-out method for a festive effect.</p>

<p>Once cut out, it’s a <strong>quick 8-10 minute bake</strong> in a 350 degree F (176 C) oven on a lined baking sheet. </p>
<p>You’re looking for a slightly puffed-up cookie with no visible brown edges (which can make the cookies dry). Once done baking, they’ll continue to firm up slightly on the baking sheet. This is one of the secrets to fluffy, tender, perfect sugar cookies every time.</p>

<span class="wprm-recipe-ingredient-notes">(like <a href="https://www.amazon.com/Watkins-Natural-Coloring-Green-Count/dp/B07KGTKWWB/?tag=minimalistbaker-20" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">Watkins</a> or <a href="https://www.amazon.com/ColorKitchen-Food-Color-Packets-0-1/dp/B074B2DW1Q/?tag=minimalistbaker-20" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">ColorKitchen</a> or my <a href="https://minimalistbaker.com/diy-natural-food-dye/" target="_blank">DIY version</a> // optional)</span>
</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>
<div class="wprm-recipe-instructions-container">
<h3 class="wprm-recipe-header">Instructions</h3>
<div class="wprm-recipe-instruction-group">
<ol class="wprm-recipe-instructions">
<li class="wprm-recipe-instruction">
<div class="wprm-recipe-instruction-text"><p>Using softened butter and a room-temperature egg is important to provide the right texture for the dough. To soften butter, set out at room temperature for about 1 hour. Resist the temptation to microwave, which will melt the butter and make it too runny. For the egg (uncracked), place in bowl and top with warm water. Let rest 10 minutes. </p></div>
</li>
<li class="wprm-recipe-instruction">
<div class="wprm-recipe-instruction-text"><p>Add softened butter to a large mixing bowl and beat with a <a href="https://aax-us-east.amazon-adsystem.com/x/c/QiE60xzvj85AaFdzQ9U5KaUAAAFt-dc6cgEAAAFKAfzdMLQ/http://www.amazon.com/dp/B000IL9GU6/?creativeASIN=B000IL9GU6&amp;linkCode=w61&amp;imprToken=FLwUM6-z1B.pqDuRagrSmw&amp;slotNum=15&amp;tag=minimalistbaker-20" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">mixer</a> on high for 30 seconds. Then add cane sugar, vanilla extract, and room temperature egg and beat on high for 1 minute, scraping down sides as needed.</p></div>
</li>
<li class="wprm-recipe-instruction">
<div class="wprm-recipe-instruction-text">Next add baking powder and sea salt and mix again to combine.</div>
</li>
<li class="wprm-recipe-instruction">
<div class="wprm-recipe-instruction-text"><p>Add almond flour, potato starch, and arrowroot starch and carefully beat on low to combine, switching to a wooden spoon as needed if it becomes difficult to mix. You’re looking for a dough that’s neither crumbly nor too soft, but easy to form in your hand without sticking. If it's too tacky at this point (which we found), add a bit more almond flour, arrowroot, and potato starch a little (~1 Tbsp) at a time and stir until a soft but moldable dough is formed (see photo).</p></div>
</li>
<li class="wprm-recipe-instruction">
<div class="wprm-recipe-instruction-text"><p>Cover and freeze for 30 minutes or refrigerate overnight. The dough is easier to roll when chilled.</p></div>
</li>
<li class="wprm-recipe-instruction">
<div class="wprm-recipe-instruction-text">In the meantime, preheat oven to 350 degrees F (176 C) and line 1-2 large baking sheets with parchment paper (the recipe as written yields 48 cookies, and we recommend baking ~12 cookies on a tray at a time).</div>
</li>
<li class="wprm-recipe-instruction">
<div class="wprm-recipe-instruction-text"><p>Once dough is chilled, dust a cutting board or clean surface with arrowroot starch and almond flour (we found the combination to work well to prevent sticking). Then roll into a circle or rectangle about 1/4-inch thick using a rolling pin (add more arrowroot and almond flour as needed to prevent sticking). We found 1/4-inch thick dough to yield fluffy cookies that weren’t too difficult to handle and baked evenly.</p></div>
</li>
<li class="wprm-recipe-instruction">
<div class="wprm-recipe-instruction-text"><p>Use cookie cutters of choice to stamp cookies, then use a thin spatula (dusted with arrowroot) to carefully transfer them onto the baking sheet. (Alternatively, you can just scoop out 1-1 ½ Tbsp amounts of dough — we like <a href="https://aax-us-east.amazon-adsystem.com/x/c/QvuVWNG4K8sCV8qL_dxXLK8AAAFt-diIeQEAAAFKAWXJQnc/https://www.amazon.com/dp/B016QDV7GK/?creativeASIN=B016QDV7GK&amp;linkCode=w61&amp;imprToken=XZJh6Wuh..q5BQJ9mBbx.w&amp;slotNum=33&amp;tag=minimalistbaker-20" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">this scoop</a> — roll into balls, and press down slightly to form a disc. Bake as instructed.)</p></div>
</li>
<li class="wprm-recipe-instruction">
<div class="wprm-recipe-instruction-text"><p>Bake cookies for 8.5 – 10 minutes. They don’t need to be golden brown at all to be baked, and they’re actually more soft and fluffy when pulled out just before browning. <strong>NOTE</strong>: We had the best success baking cookies 1 tray at a time versus 2 (or more).</p></div>
</li>
<li class="wprm-recipe-instruction">
<div class="wprm-recipe-instruction-text">Remove from oven and let cool for 5-10 minutes on tray, then transfer to a serving platter or cooling rack, being careful not to stack them until completely cooled.</div>
</li>
<li class="wprm-recipe-instruction">
<div class="wprm-recipe-instruction-text"><p><em><strong>Optional:</strong></em> To make frosting, add softened dairy-free butter to a mixing bowl and whisk or beat until soft and fluffy — about 1 minute. Then add the vanilla and sifted powdered sugar in 1/2-cup (56 g) increments and mix. Continue adding powdered sugar until you have spreadable frosting (we used about 2 cups powdered sugar // amount as original recipe is written // adjust if altering batch size). For thinner frosting, add dairy-free milk 1 tsp at a time to thin. If it becomes too thin, add more powdered sugar. If it becomes too thick, add more almond milk. Add natural food coloring or powdered dye of choice at this time (or make our <a href="https://minimalistbaker.com/vegan-matcha-buttercream-frosting-1-bowl/" target="_blank">matcha variation</a>!). Once completely cooled, frost cookies or leave plain. We decorated with some naturally-dyed sugar sprinkles <a href="https://www.amazon.com/Silicone-Baking-Non-Stick-Cookie-Sheet/dp/B004T0DEG4/?creativeASIN=B00OF1M3IC&amp;linkCode=w61&amp;imprToken=TxMGW9fVlzArg3p92c9Tdw&amp;slotNum=0&amp;tag=minimalistbaker-20" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">like these</a>.</p></div>
</li>
<li class="wprm-recipe-instruction">
<div class="wprm-recipe-instruction-text"><p><strong>Storage</strong>: Once cooled, store leftover cookies covered at room temperature or in the refrigerator for 3-4 days or in the freezer up to 1 month.</p></div>
</li>
</ol>
</div>
</div>

<div class="wprm-recipe-notes-container">
<h3 class="wprm-recipe-header">Notes</h3>
<p>*We haven’t tested this recipe with coconut oil but suspect it wouldn’t work. Therefore, we highly recommend sticking with vegan butter (Earth Balance brand sticks) or organic dairy butter if not vegan.<br/>*If you’d like to cut back on sugar, sub 1/3 cup of the sugar with 1 packet (~1/4 tsp) stevia extract (amount as original recipe is written // adjust if altering batch size). I like Trader Joe’s brand. This will make the cookies softer, so you may need to add more potato starch, almond flour, and/or arrowroot starch to help thicken the dough.<br/>*The egg can potentially be substituted for aquafaba (chickpea brine) in this recipe. But we haven’t tested it and can’t guarantee the results. In its place, you can also try subbing 1/4 cup pumpkin purée or 1 egg replacer (amounts as original recipe is written // adjust if altering batch size). But we haven’t tested it with a flax egg or any substitute and can’t guarantee the results.<br/>*If not gluten-free, you can sub the almond flour, potato starch, and arrowroot starch with unbleached all-purpose flour, although we haven’t tested it and can’t guarantee the results.<br/>*You can make the cookie dough ahead of time and refrigerate up to 2-3 days in advance. Simply let the dough rest at room temperature for 30 minutes before scooping/rolling out and baking. It should be soft and moldable.<br/>*Nutrition information is a rough estimate calculated without frosting.</p>

<div class="comment-content">
<p>These turned out well even though I messed up the dough a bit. I used corn starch instead of potato, Miyoko’s butter, and a 1-egg equivalent using the Bob’s Red Mill Egg Replacer. The first time I mixed everything, the dough was so dry that it didn’t come together on it’s own (I was using an electric hand mixer on low), but in retrospect maybe I should have mixed using my hands or something. It just seemed too dehydrated! To try and get the dough to come together I used maybe a tablespoon of almond milk and a tablespoon of neutral oil (to prevent the dough from becoming less fluffy from the additional liquid, which I find can happen sometimes). I also rolled out using cornstarch. The extra oil made the dough a bit of a pain to work with–it was just too soft and kept absorbing the starch–but it still held together well enough for me to slide them onto a baking sheet. Either way these taste delicious, the texture is lovely, and because it made like 34 cookies I’m going to have some fun with the vegan matcha frosting. I’d love to experiment with these a little further!</p>
